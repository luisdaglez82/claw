# What Is a Claw Strategy and Why Your Business Needs One Now

At GTC 2026, Jensen Huang made it plain: *"Every company needs an OpenClaw strategy — an agentic systems strategy. Just as we all needed a Linux strategy, an internet strategy, a mobile and cloud strategy."* He wasn't pitching a product. He was describing a shift in business infrastructure that's already underway.

If you run a business — a practice, a shop, an agency, a solo operation — a Claw Strategy is your plan for putting AI agents to work inside your operations. Not someday. Now.

## Where the Name Comes From

OpenClaw is an open-source platform that acts as an operating system for personal AI agents. Huang called it "the beginning of a new renaissance in software." NVIDIA built NemoClaw on top of it — an enterprise-grade version with security and compliance features baked in.

But a Claw Strategy isn't about one platform. It's the strategic decision to build your business with AI agents as operational teammates. You already have a marketing strategy and a hiring strategy. Now you need an agent strategy.

## What Changes When You Have One

Huang projected that NVIDIA will go from 75,000 human employees to working alongside 7.5 million AI agents — a 100-to-1 ratio. He even floated giving employees a token budget on top of their salary so they can deploy their own productivity agents.

For a small or mid-size business, the scale is different but the logic holds. You don't need millions of agents. You need a handful that do real work:

- An agent that handles customer inquiries at 2 AM.
- An agent that scores and prioritizes your inbound leads.
- An agent that generates weekly sales reports without being asked.
- An agent that books appointments and sends reminders automatically.

Each agent runs on tokens — the processing units of AI. The cost is measurable, predictable, and in most cases far cheaper than the human hours they replace.

## What It Actually Costs

You don't need NVIDIA's budget. Here are real prices as of April 2026:

**Claude by Anthropic (API pricing):**

- Haiku 4.5: $1 per million input tokens, $5 output. Best for high-volume simple tasks.
- Sonnet 4.6: $3 input, $15 output per million tokens. Strong balance of capability and cost.
- Opus 4.6: $5 input, $25 output per million tokens. For complex reasoning tasks.

Concrete example: processing 10,000 customer support tickets with Opus 4.6 costs roughly $37. That's less than a single day of a support rep's salary in most US markets.

Batch processing cuts those prices in half. Prompt caching can reduce input costs by up to 90% for repeated context.

**Automation platforms:**

- n8n: free and open-source (self-hosted). Cloud from $24/month.
- Make: from $9/month for 10,000 operations.
- Zapier: from $19.99/month.

## Why Now, Not Later

Huang quantified the demand: over $1 trillion in AI computing spend projected through 2027. Businesses that integrate agents now will operate with lower costs, faster response times, and higher capacity than those who wait.

This isn't about chasing trends. It's an infrastructure decision. Having a website stopped being optional fifteen years ago. Having AI agents working in your business is on the same trajectory — and the entry cost is far lower than most people assume.

You don't need a team of engineers. You need to identify which processes in your business can be automated, pick the right tools, and start with one agent solving one real problem.

## What You Can Do Today

1. **List three repetitive tasks in your business** that eat more than 2 hours per week: answering FAQs, scheduling appointments, generating reports, sorting emails. Those are your first agent candidates.
2. **Sign up at [claude.ai](https://claude.ai)** and test one of those tasks manually. Paste a real customer email and ask Claude to draft the response. Measure time saved and quality. Real data beats speculation.
3. **Read NVIDIA's OpenClaw announcement** at [nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/ai-agents](https://nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/ai-agents) to understand where the industry is heading. Skip the technical specs — focus on the enterprise use cases section.
